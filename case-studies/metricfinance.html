Metric Finance

AI-powered equity research for everyday Indian investors. Users can enter any NSE or BSE ticker and receive a clear, structured research report in seconds.

150+ users in 10 days
1,000+ analyses in 2 weeks
1.5M launch impressions

Problem & Insight

I identified a clear gap in the Indian retail investing market. India has 80M+ demat account holders, with 30M+ new accounts opening every year, but most tools still assume users already know how to interpret financial data.

Existing platforms were either built for professional analysts, where raw numbers are presented with limited explanation, or for broad screening, where first-time investors are overwhelmed by disconnected metrics. Everyday investors did not need more data; they needed research that explained what the data meant before they committed capital.

Strategy & Market Position

I positioned Metric Finance as a plain-language research layer for Indian equities. The core promise was simple: enter any NSE or BSE ticker and receive a complete, readable research report within seconds.

The product was designed for first-time and retail investors actively researching stocks, not for day traders or institutional analysts. That focus shaped every product decision. The goal was not to maximize trading speed or technical complexity; it was to help users build confidence, understand risk, and know what to investigate next.

Product Design

I designed the product around one focused flow: search for a ticker or company name, generate an AI research report, and share the report through a persistent page.

Each report covers eight dimensions: company overview, price action, peer comparison, fundamentals, news sentiment, technical signals, key risks, and a contrarian view with both bull and bear cases. Every section was written in plain language so users could move from curiosity to a practical understanding of the investment case in under a minute.

The central product insight was that interpretation is more valuable than raw data. A P/E ratio or volume spike means little without context, so Metric Finance translated each metric into a clear explanation of why it mattered.

Tech & Architecture

I built Metric Finance using Next.js 15, React 19, TypeScript, Tailwind CSS 4, Neon Postgres, and Drizzle ORM. The architecture was designed around reliability, speed, shareability, measurement, and a future path toward licensed production market data.

Structured report generation

Report generation used strict structured schemas rather than unstructured text responses. I defined exact fields for the executive summary, before-you-buy brief, metric interpretation, valuation language, earnings read, market-timing risks, and plain-English explanations. This made every report section predictable, displayable, and reliable in the frontend.

Market data abstraction

The market data layer was built behind provider abstractions. The MVP used Yahoo Finance as a temporary bridge while preserving adapters for OpenAlgo and Upstox-style data. This prevented vendor lock-in and kept the product ready to migrate toward licensed market data as it matured.

Report caching

Postgres and Drizzle persisted generated reports, generation jobs, source data, timestamps, and metadata. Reports were cached by ticker for 24 hours, improving repeat load times, reducing unnecessary AI generation costs, and keeping shared report links stable.

Analytics, auth, and retention

Product analytics tracked the full funnel: landing views, search opens, search submits, report views, shares, analysis runs, authentication events, and watchlist saves. Google authentication through NextAuth stayed optional for core research, while signed-in users could save watchlists. This created the first retention loop by giving users a reason to return to their personal research set.

Execution

Phase 1: Core job-to-be-done

I started with the core user job: search a ticker, receive readable analysis, and understand the stock quickly. The first version prioritized end-to-end search, report generation, and shareable report pages.

Phase 2: Data grounding and caching

I then added market-data snapshots, source-data storage, 24-hour report reuse, generation-job tracking, and fallback behavior. These systems moved the product from a demo into a more reliable research experience.

Phase 3: Product analytics

I instrumented acquisition, search intent, report engagement, sharing, generation, and authentication lifecycle events. This created a measurement foundation for funnel analysis, activation, retention, and cost-per-use tracking.

Phase 4: Watchlists

Watchlists became the first signed-in retention feature, giving users a practical reason to return and build a personal research workflow.

Phase 5: UX polish

I refined the dark finance-dashboard interface, homepage messaging, report scanning layouts, mobile spacing, responsive sections, and watchlist calls to action.

Launch & Growth

I launched Metric Finance in June 2026 with a dual-channel growth strategy combining organic Twitter distribution and paid creator partnerships.

For the organic launch, I wrote a product announcement focused on the problem, the user pain, and the promise of faster stock research. I then distributed it through high-relevance lifestyle and finance accounts in the startup niche, generating 1.5M impressions during the launch campaign.

When organic engagement began showing signs of audience fatigue, I tested paid creator content to improve conversion quality. I partnered with two lifestyle creators to produce testimonial-style UGC showing how Metric Finance reduced the friction of stock research. This channel delivered double-digit conversion rates and outperformed organic Twitter referral traffic, showing that social proof converted more effectively than reach alone.

Key Learnings

This project reinforced the value of solving a specific problem for a clearly defined market. By focusing on one outcome, helping everyday investors understand Indian stocks in plain language, every feature had a clear purpose.

I learned that interpretation is more defensible than data access. Market data is widely available, but translating that data into useful context is where the product creates value.

I also learned that organic reach and paid conversion serve different roles. Twitter created awareness at scale, while creator partnerships converted interested audiences at stronger rates. The stronger playbook combined broad distribution with high-trust creator proof.

From a technical perspective, structured AI output became one of the most important reliability decisions. Exact schemas reduced frontend risk and made the report experience consistent across users and tickers.

Finally, caching proved to be a product decision, not just an engineering optimization. Faster report loads improved repeat usage, lower generation costs made growth more sustainable, and stable report links made the product more trustworthy.
